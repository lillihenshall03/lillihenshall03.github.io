MEASURED "responsibility"
Reflection
Western approaches to understanding are heavily reliant on scientific methodologies. This is a linear progression where identifiable steps lead to the collation of observable results (Martin 2016). As an example, Double Diamond is a design method which was popularised by European regions. It prioritises objectivity and the efficiency of method-following, whilst negating proccesses of relationality and personhood.This limits the inclusion of Indigenous ways of knowing.
The Millenium Seed Bank Project was initiated in 1996 with the aim of resolving large scale biodiversity loss. The enterprise yielded positive results and expanded into a research bank to document plant species. These species are collated at the Royal Botanic Gardens (Kew). Though there is nothing inherently wrong with this ongoing research, it must be noted that biodiversity loss is exacerbated by Western practices such as industrialisation and mass consumerism. I reference this within my visual strategy by pairing the structural makeup of plants and industrial structures. These issues must be addressed as interdependent in order to produce effective solutions. However, it is unlikely that this will be considered in the near future as design and research structures continue to operate from colonial foundations.
As it would be an act of appropriation to attempt to replicate Indigenous knowledges, I instead recognise my personhood and the unconscious biases I carry. By critiquiing the Western frameworks that have shaped my processes of learning and understanding, I can effectively participate in decolonising design.
Understanding
Planetary Aesthesis, Not Animal Citizens
Returning to the question of accountability, one can conclude that it, along with the notion of free will it rests on, belong to the outline of the Cartesian subject whose historical invention must be held responsible for the conversion of the natural world, and its black(ened) communities, to borrow Zakiyyah Iman Jackson’s term, into a set of objects for “Man’s” extraction, enjoyment, consumption, domination, and exhaustion. Is it possible to have responsibility (or autonomous, non-individualizing forms of accountability) without this Cartesian model of the subject and all the biopolitical violence such a subject has historically implied for the excluded, the nonhuman animal, or more-than-human worlds?
This seems crucially important from the point of view of an ecological ethics in the present conjuncture, when all human activity seems to constitute an abuse of “planetary space.” Furthermore, how can one allocate ecological accountability and adjudicate on matters such as climate debt without mistakenly falling into this Cartesian framework? After all, this is the very framework that provides an alibi for the most egregious perpetrators of global ecocide because, through its universalization of a genre-specific human ontology, it accuses the “human species” of ecocide without specifying the capitalist economic system in which we are historically, not naturally or essentially, caught.
